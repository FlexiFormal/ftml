Abstract.
The flexiformalist program promises to combine the distinct advantages of both machine-oriented formal and human-oriented informal mathematics, by representing both in an interleaved manner in the same document, but so far lacked the necesssary comprehensive software support to do so in practice.

Introduction

Recognizing the discrepancy between formal and informal mathematics as well as formal and informal mathematical practice, the flexiformalist program [Koh13] calls for annotating informal, human-oriented documents with partially formalized (and modular, reusable, shareable, distibuted, etc.) content commons (a.k.a. a domain model). This promises to mitigate the difficulties and required expertise associated with fully formalizing mathematical results, while retaining many of the advantages of formality; most notably, still allowing for most useful software support (apart from full verification), such as disambiguation, interlinking, lookup, documentation, semantic search, etc.
Naturally though, fulfilling this promise in practice requires comprehensive software support for the entire producer-to-consumer pipeline of flexiformal documents, spanning an entire ecosystem of associated activities starting with
1. authoring documents,
2. authoring/generating/extracting the content commons,
3. curating the domain model FAIRly (findably, accessibly, interoperably, reusably), and
4. viewing/sharing/publishing the flexiformal documents, with
5. the promised added value software services integrated.
The end result of the process are active documents that benefit readers by making the knowledge contained in them (and prerequisite knowledge) more explicit, accessible, and easier and faster to learn.
All of the steps above have been realized (usually) in relative isolation, with the result that following the entire pipeline from authoring to viewing documents has been a daunting process, involving multiple partially instable systems at precise versions, with each step being error prone, difficult to set up, maintain and debug.

The FTML/OMDoc Ontology

The primary shortcomings of the Mmt-centered previous incarnation of our flexiformal document ecosystem were the result of a lack of support for documents and document fragments, which [Ber+23] calls the formulation model. In the terminology of the tetrapod model [Car+21], we would say that Mmt focuses on the inference dimension and lacks support for the narration dimension. We consequently call the ontology surrounding document fragments the narrative model. As the narrative model is the central novelty of FTML/ 𝖥 𝖫
∀
𝖬 ∫ compared to the previous pipeline, we will focus on that in subsection 2.3
 and only briefly cover the domain models in subsection 2.2
.

The domain model serves as the (largely) language-and-author-independent “platonic” ontology of knowledge, that we want to flexiformally annotate documents with.
The narrative model serves two purposes: Firstly, to associate parts of a document with its flexiformal semantics, and secondly, to segment documents into semantically meaningful units to a) fuel services, and b) modularly compose them into new documents. Consider for example the piece of text:
Definition 3.14
A natural number 𝑛 is called prime, if 𝑛 > 1 and whenever 𝑚 | 𝑛 , then 𝑚 = 1 or 𝑚 = 𝑛 .
Firstly, we want to associate parts of the document with concepts from the domain model; i.e. we want to have “natural number”, “ > ”, “ | ” etc. be annotated with the corresponding symbols. Beyond that, however, we may also want to annotate the entire paragraph as being a definition, the definiendum of which is the symbol “prime”, and the definiens of which is represented by the fragment “ 𝑛 > 1 and whenever 𝑚 | 𝑛 , then 𝑚 = 1 or 𝑚 = 𝑛 ”. This enables reader-oriented services such as definitions-on-hover for “natural number”, customized highlighting of definienda or definientia, indexing of all definitions in the document, etc.
Secondly however, knowing this is a definition, we want to be able to use it in situations where a definition of the symbol “prime” is required – e.g. when hovering over the word prime in some other document, or explicitly include it in a new introductory document on number theory. This entails that such snippets (as e.g. definitions) need to be able to dynamically adapt to some document context, e.g. by using the correct number for number-labeled paragraphs (“Definition 3.14”).
Note that even though we deliberately distinguish between domain and narrative models, we want flexiformally annotated documents to serve as the source for both, and thus expect domain model entities (i.e. modules and their declarations) to appear anywhere within documents. If so, they are extracted and stored/curated separately, but a corresponding reference is recorded in the narrative model, so that e.g. document viewer services can be aware of them.

Domain Model

For the domain model, we largely follow Mmt/OMDoc, and as such OpenMath, with the notable distinction that we provide first-class support for structures as record( type)s, conservative extensions, and opaque (flexiformal) expressions. All three of these have existed previously in Mmt, encoded as plain OpenMath, and as such are not described in detail here.
The domain model consists of modules, which are named (by a Module URI) lists of the following declarations:
Nested
modules
add arbitrary levels of additional namespacing.
Symbols
are named (by a Symbol URI) concepts; analogous to e.g. Mmt constants; A symbol has optional type and definiens components, which are terms represented as (equivalent to) OpenMath expressions.
Structures
are named (by a Symbol URI) dependent record types with manifest fields. They are represented via theories-as-types [MRK18] as module-like lists of arbitrary declarations, the symbols of which represent the fields of the record type. As such, a structure is (or generates) both a symbol with Symbol URI <base>&m=module&s=name, as well as a (nested) module with Module URI <base>&m=module/name.
Includes
of a module 𝑀 make all declarations in 𝑀 or transitively imported into 𝑀 available to the current module.
Morphisms
correspond to theory morphisms as in [RK13], i.e. “imports with judgment-preserving modifications”. In a formal context such as Mmt, morphisms are well-understood regarding their behaviour; for flexiformal content, we are actively experimenting with ways to extend their definition to additionally cover “narrative” use cases (see e.g. [KS24]).
Conservative
extensions
allow for extending existing modules and (much more commonly) structures with new declarations; provided all of them are defined.
All of the above declarations are described in more detail on the basis of
S
T
E
X in [CICM22c] and/or [KM] and shown to be equivalent to Mmt/OMDoc in [CICM22c].

The narrative model consists of documents, which (we assume) correspond to single source files (e.g. .tex, .md, .docx), are named by Document URIs and contain a list of narrative elements. In the following, we will discuss the most central of these, but we naturally keep the set of types of narrative elements open for future extensions.
A document include references another document (via its Document URI), indicating that the referenced document should be inserted in full at the position of this document include.
